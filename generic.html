Creative Philosophy

Exploring the state of consciousness, by expressing the dichotomy between the deterministic and stochastic states of reality. The intention of my creative philosophy is to capture the transcendental beauty of the universe through the lens of the observer. As such, we venture together through the realm of the unseen.

Photo by: Yasin Arıbuğa, Unsplash License

The Creative Act (1957) – Marcel Duchamp

“Let us consider two important factors, the two poles of the creation of art: the artist on the one hand, and on the other the spectator who later becomes the posterity.

To all appearances, the artist acts like a mediumistic being who, from the labyrinth beyond time and space, seeks his way out to a clearing. If we give the attributes of a medium to the artist, we must then deny him the state of consciousness on the esthetic plane about what he is doing or why he is doing it. All his decisions in the artistic execution of the work rest with pure intuition and cannot be translated into a self-analysis, spoken, or written, or even thought out.

T.S. Eliot, in his essay on “Tradition and Individual Talent”, writes: “The more perfect the artist, the more completely separate in him will be the man who suffers and the mind which creates; the more perfectly will the mind digest and transmute the passions which are its material.”

Millions of artists create; only a few thousands are discussed or accepted by the spectator and many less again are consecrated by posterity. In the last analysis, the artist may shout from all the rooftops that he is a genius: he will have to wait for the verdict of the spectator in order that his declarations take a social value and that, finally, posterity includes him in the primers of Artist History.

I know that this statement will not meet with the approval of many artists who refuse this mediumistic role and insist on the validity of their awareness in the creative act – yet art history has consistently decided upon the virtues of a work of art through considerations completely divorced from the rationalized explanations of the artist.

If the artist, as a human being, full of the best intentions toward himself and the whole world, plays no role at all in the judgment of his own work, how can one describe the phenomenon which prompts the spectator to react critically to the work of art?

In other words, how does this reaction come about?

This phenomenon is comparable to a transference from the artist to the spectator in the form of an esthetic osmosis taking place through the inert matter, such as pigment, piano or marble.

But before we go further, I want to clarify our understanding of the word ‘art’ - to be sure, without any attempt at a definition. What I have in mind is that art may be bad, good or indifferent, but whatever adjective is used, we must call it art, and bad art is still art in the same way that a bad emotion is still an emotion.

Therefore, when I refer to ‘art coefficient’, it will be understood that I refer not only to great art, but I am trying to describe the subjective mechanism which produces art in the raw state – à l’état brut – bad, good, or indifferent.

In the creative act, the artist goes from intention to realization through a chain of totally subjective reactions.

His struggle toward the realization is a series of efforts, pains, satisfaction, refusals, decisions, which also cannot and must not be fully self-conscious, at least on the esthetic plane.

The result of this struggle is a difference between the intention and its realization, a difference which the artist is not aware of. Consequently, in the chain of reactions accompanying the creative act, a link is missing. This gap, representing the inability of the artist to express fully his intention, this difference between what he intended to realize and did realize, is the personal ‘art coefficient’ contained in the work.

In other words, the personal ‘art coefficient’ is like an arithmetical relation between the unexpressed but intended and the unintentionally expressed.

To avoid a misunderstanding, we must remember that this ‘art coefficient’ is a personal expression of art à l’état brut, that is, still in a raw state, which must be ‘refined’ as pure sugar from molasses by the spectator; the digit of this coefficient has no bearing whatsoever on his verdict.

The creative act takes another aspect when the spectator experiences the phenomenon of transmutation: through the change from inert matter into a work of art, an actual transubtantiation has taken place, and the role of the spectator is to determine the weight of the work on the esthetic scale.

All in all, the creative act is not performed by the artist alone; the spectator brings the work in contact with the external world by deciphering and interpreting its inner qualification and thus adds his contribution to the creative act. This becomes even more obvious when posterity gives a final verdict and sometimes rehabilitates forgotten artists.

Core Values

The basis of my philosophy and artistic perspective is heavily inspired by the 'Creative Act (1957)' by Marcel Duchamp.

“The only thing I do know, is that I know nothing,” famously said by Socrates contains invaluable wisdom. A motto of humility, especially regarding certainty of one’s knowledge is admirable in an individual. The universe we live in is strange and largely misunderstood. Any time humanity has become too presumptuous about the state of its own knowledge, the descendants of prior generations correct inaccuracies and then laugh at the ignorance of the past.

However, as we uncover more information, we are left with more questions than answers. What is the underlying causal structure of the universe that we as a species so diligently attempt to identify? What is the role of the observer in consciousness? Does agency influence the perceived state of reality? From quantum mechanics, we know that the behavior of light depending on the presence of an observer will take on wave or particle like properties. Art, and its interpretation behaves in a similar way.

The concern is that we exist as a society who has drawn a clear line between art and mathematics. However, the geometries that can be produced using the tools of mathematics are nevertheless visually appealing. What is this effect of geometry on consciousness and why can it be consistently found throughout nature, architecture, and art? From a scientific perspective one might ask if this can be measured. Alternatively, a differing perspective might respond with how it’s a feeling, where intuitively you just know.

Whether the tool used is a generative adversarial network deep learning algorithm, or simply mud and sticks, the manner in which projects are perceived rests on the feelings of the observer. Through compassion, love, and understanding we can grow as individuals, by allowing each other to address the internal suffering in oneself. At the intersection between two extremes whether that be pleasure and pain; reasoning and intuition; or life and death, lies inner healing and true beauty.

I intend to operate within this domain, and to embed these core values into my artistic endeavors. Through combination of art, science, mathematics, and technology I believe that the task of creating fundamentally beautiful art should be investigated.

In this form of expression, there is a distinction between the art itself and the artist. In the artistic execution, all decisions in the creations must be made purely by intuition. Logical intuition derived from mathematical reasoning can be performed using organic or artificial intelligence. The components pertaining to science, mathematics, and technology are developed with varying degrees of organic or human intuition, undisclosed to the observer.

The value of the art is left to the subjective feelings of the observer. Following this interaction, the “Art” no longer is no longer represented solely by its physical value, the emotion of each observer becomes part of the art itself. Conversely, whether this abstraction is accepted or denied, is subject only to the observer.

I believe, that through the combination of physics and mathematics, with the artist themselves as the medium by which the transmutation of inert matter into art is achieved; we may better understand each other as individuals.

The origin of the word philosophy comes from the Greek word φιλοσοφία / Philosophia, which means “Love of Wisdom.” Although, this thought experiment bears no physical significance, it was done out of passion. Despite any wisdom that may be acquired along the journey of life, when death embraces me, I will be soothed by the fact that all I know is that I know nothing; however, I did what was within my power to lead a good life. This is what art means me, and this is the meaning of my philosophy.
